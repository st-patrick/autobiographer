# Synthesis: Best practices for interviewing about life stories

Based on 5 cached sources covering: oral history methodology (institutional),
academic narrative psychology (McAdams), reminiscence therapy (clinical),
practical parent-interviewing (popular), and sensory memory recovery (memoir).

## What everyone agrees on

### 1. Open-ended, scene-specific questions beat abstract ones

Every source emphasizes the same thing: don't ask "were you happy as a kid?"
ask "describe a specific day from when you were eight."

McAdams' protocol formalizes this with the **"key scene" structure** — every
prompt asks for ONE specific incident with details: what happened, when, where,
who was involved, what they were thinking and feeling. *(See `sources/mcadams-life-story-interview.md`.)*

The OHA best practices echo this: "Ask questions that require more of an
answer than 'yes' or 'no.' Start with 'Why, How, Where, What kind of...'" *(See `sources/oral-history-association-best-practices.md`.)*

### 2. Sensory triggers unlock memories that questions alone can't reach

Reminiscence therapy research shows the most effective triggers are
**memory triggers (used in 59% of clinical studies)** — specifically photos,
music, objects, smells, tastes. *(See `sources/reminiscence-therapy-narrative-analysis.md`.)*

The memoir technique adds: write in **first-person present tense** ("I am 8.
I am running...") to make memory more immediately accessible. *(See `sources/sensory-memory-prompts.md`.)*

A "sensory walk" prompt structure works: "Close your eyes. You're [age]. What
do you smell? What do you hear? What's the temperature?"

### 3. The "reminiscence bump" — ages 10–30 are most accessible

Both reminiscence therapy and narrative psychology converge: people most
vividly recall events from **ages 10 to 30**, especially late teens and early
twenties. This is the period most amenable to memory recovery via prompts.
Earlier years (0–5) are largely unrecoverable directly — they require
**third-party interviews** (parents, older siblings).

### 4. Structure beats free-form for whole-life coverage

McAdams' "life as a book with chapters" framing asks the subject to do the
meta-organization themselves before drilling into specific scenes. This is
more powerful than chronological year-by-year because:
- The subject's own chapter boundaries reveal what they consider significant
- Forces them to think about transitions (the "between" moments often hold
  the most insight)
- Prevents the trap of cataloguing without reflecting

### 5. Six questions in forty minutes — go slow

StoryCorps' operating rule: **6 questions in a 40-minute interview**, ~7 minutes
per question. Each question is a doorway into a long story, not a quick answer.
Modern apps tend to ask too many small questions; the gold standard is fewer,
deeper. *(See `sources/storycorps-great-questions-summary.md`.)*

## Where sources disagree (or focus differently)

- **OHA / Oral History**: heavy on archival and consent procedures, light on
  question content. Treats the interview as primary-source documentation.
- **McAdams / Academic**: structured protocol (~2 hours), heavy on identity
  themes and life arc. Treats the interview as a measurement instrument.
- **Reminiscence therapy / Clinical**: heavy on triggers and emotional safety,
  designed for vulnerable populations (dementia patients). Treats the
  interview as therapeutic intervention.
- **StoryCorps / Popular**: warm and curated, emphasizes the relationship
  between interviewer and interviewee. Treats the interview as a gift exchange.
- **Memoir prompts / Creative**: solo, sensory-focused, no interviewer.
  Treats writing as memory excavation.

These don't conflict — they're complementary lenses.

## Frameworks worth borrowing

### A. The "key scene" framework (McAdams)

Eight specific scenes that, together, form a life portrait:
1. Peak experience (highest high)
2. Nadir experience (lowest low)
3. Turning point
4. Earliest memory
5. Important childhood scene
6. Important adolescent scene
7. Important adult scene
8. Other vivid scene

This is dramatically more useful than year-by-year for users who already have
many entries — it surfaces the *meaningful* moments.

### B. The chapter/book framing (McAdams)

"If your life were a book, what are the chapter titles? What's each one
about? How do you transition from one to the next?" — this is a great
"warm-up" question before getting into specifics.

### C. The sensory walk (memoir technique)

"Close your eyes. You are [age], [year]. You're standing in [familiar place].
What do you smell? What do you hear? Who's nearby? What's the temperature?
What are you wearing?"

This is a structured way to reactivate episodic memory using all five senses
in sequence. Far more effective than "tell me about that year."

### D. The third-party interview (35 questions framework)

For periods you can't access yourself (infancy, very early childhood),
interview someone who was there. Specific prompts to ask the third party:
- "What was [I] like at age [X]?"
- "What scared [me]?"
- "What made [me] laugh?"
- "What were the routines? What did our days look like?"
- "What was happening in your life at the time?"

### E. The wisdom-transfer question (StoryCorps)

"If you could talk to a younger version of yourself, what would you say?"
A reflective, generative question that produces insight without requiring
factual recall — useful as an interview closer or for years where memories
are sparse.

## Open questions / what's still unclear

- **No source addressed text-only interviewing** (vs. spoken). All assume
  voice or presence. The transition from spoken to written may change
  what works (writing is slower; can be more reflective; loses tone of voice).
- **No source quantified question quality.** Lots of opinion, no measurement
  of "this question produces 3x more recall than that one."
- **Cultural variance** is mostly absent. Most sources are US-centric.
- **The reminiscence bump's underlying mechanism** isn't deeply explained
  in our sources — would need a follow-up search if we want to know *why*.
