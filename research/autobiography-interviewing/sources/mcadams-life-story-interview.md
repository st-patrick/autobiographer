# Dan McAdams' Life Story Interview Protocol Structure

## Main Protocol Sections

1. **Life Chapters** — ask the person to describe their life as a book with chapters (titles, summaries, transitions)
2. **Critical Events** — eight specific scenes (see below)
3. **Life Challenge** — major struggles, problems, difficulties
4. **Alternative Futures for the Life Story** — possible selves, hopes, fears
5. **Life Theme** — central message or idea running throughout the life story
6. **Other** — anything else the narrator wants to add

## Eight Key Scene Types with Question Wording

**Event #1: Peak Experience**
"A peak experience would be a high point in your life story -- perhaps the high point...Please describe in some detail a peak experience, or something like it, that you have experienced some time in your past."

**Event #2: Nadir Experience**
"A 'nadir' is a low point...Thinking back over your life, try to remember a specific experience in which you felt extremely negative emotions, such as despair, disillusionment, terror, guilt, etc."

**Event #3: Turning Point**
"In looking back on one's life, it is often possible to identify certain key 'turning points'...Please identify a particular episode in your life story that you now see as a turning point."

**Event #4: Earliest Memory**
"Think back now to your childhood, as far back as you can go. Please choose a relatively clear memory from your earliest years and describe it in some detail."

**Event #5: Important Childhood Scene**
"Describe another memory from childhood...that stands out in your mind as especially important or significant."

**Event #6: Important Adolescent Scene**
"Describe a specific event from your teen-aged years that stands out as being especially important or significant."

**Event #7: Important Adult Scene**
"Describe a specific event from your adult years (age 21 and beyond) that stands out as being especially important or significant."

**Event #8: Other Important Scene**
"Describe one more event, from any point in your life, that stands out in your memory as being especially important or significant."

## Notes for our use

The "book with chapters" framing is powerful because it asks the subject to do the meta-organization themselves before drilling into specific scenes. Worth borrowing in some form.

The 8-scene structure is striking: only 4 of the 8 are positive ("peak", "important childhood/adolescent/adult"), while the others probe difficulty, change, and earliest memory. This balances the typical journaling tendency to gravitate toward either pure positivity or pure complaint.

Each "scene" question asks for **one specific incident** with details: "what happened, when and where it happened, who was involved, and what they were thinking and feeling." This level of detail is what unlocks narrative memory vs. abstract self-description.
