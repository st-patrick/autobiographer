# Reminiscence Therapy Components and Typologies

## Key Components Identified

The analysis identified **13 reminiscence therapy components**:

- Reality orientation (time/place)
- Themes (childhood, jobs, marriage)
- Memory triggers (photos, songs, objects)
- Life stages exploration
- Life story books
- Family involvement (occasional or dedicated sessions)
- Music interventions
- Memory boxes
- Activities (discussion, dance, dramatization)
- Staff training
- Song closure to sessions
- Guided reminiscence using life story materials

The most frequently used were **memory triggers (59% of studies)** and **themes (55%)**.

## Typology of Reminiscence

The review identifies four approaches:

1. **Simple Reminiscence**: Described as involving "autobiographical storytelling" where individuals share personal memories informally
2. **Life Review**: A structured, evaluative approach typically conducted individually, exploring life experiences chronologically with therapeutic support
3. **Life Review Therapy**: Involves re-evaluating negative memories and reframing them positively
4. **Life Story Work**: Creating biographical narratives, often resulting in "life story books"

## Memory-Triggering Techniques

Documented techniques include:

- Personal photographs and albums
- Recordings and music
- Newspaper clippings
- Objects and artifacts
- Visual cues and slides
- Seasonal/thematic prompts
- Old tools and domestic items

## Notes for our use

The strongest empirical insight here is **what works as triggers**: photos, music, objects, and themes. For a text-only app, we can't use literal photos/music, but we can:
- Reference popular music of the year
- Reference cultural events (movies, news)
- Use object-anchored questions ("describe your bedroom")
- Use sensory-anchored questions

The **typology** is also useful:
- "Simple reminiscence" = the existing interview mode
- "Life review" = a structured chronological mode (worth adding)
- "Life story work" = the autobiography itself (already what they're doing)

The "reminiscence bump" concept (mentioned elsewhere but absent here): people most vividly recall events from ages 10–30, especially late teens/early 20s. This is the period most amenable to recovery.
