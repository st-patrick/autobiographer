# Writing Prompts and Memory-Activation Techniques

## Sensory-Based Memory Triggers

The article emphasizes using sensory language as a core technique. When recalling memories, writers should engage:

- **Visual**: "What did you see"
- **Auditory**: "hear"
- **Olfactory**: "smell"
- **Gustatory**: "taste"
- **Tactile**: "feel"

The author notes that incorporating these descriptive elements helps individuals "vividly recall the memory and make it feel more real."

## Six Key Implementation Tips

1. **Environmental Setup**: Create a conducive atmosphere through quiet spaces, candles, or calming background sounds to facilitate deeper introspection.
2. **Freewriting Practice**: "Set a timer for 10-20 minutes and start writing without stopping" while ignoring grammar or structure concerns.
3. **Temporal Perspective**: Writing memories "in the first-person present tense" makes experiences feel more immediate and emotionally accessible.
4. **Authentic Expression**: Remove self-censorship by writing as if "no one will ever read your words."
5. **Consistent Routine**: Establish dedicated writing sessions to develop sustainable writing habits.
6. **Collaborative Recall**: Engage family and friends to piece together fragmented or buried memories through their perspectives.

## Prompt Function

Writing prompts operate as "portals to the past" by presenting focused questions that unlock subconscious memories while simultaneously overcoming writer's block through directed inquiry.

## Notes for our use

The big insight: **writing in present tense** ("I am 8. I am running across the playground...") makes memory more accessible than past tense recall. We could prompt this: "Write this as if it's happening right now."

The **6 senses** framework is concrete and actionable. Current interview questions occasionally use sensory language but not systematically. Worth turning into a structured "sensory walk" prompt: "You're [age]. Close your eyes. What do you smell? What do you hear? What can you taste in your mouth?"

The **collaborative recall** point is exactly what the user is doing with his mom — and it's evidence-backed.
